Topic 01

Turn the Clock Back to Spin-Off

Why $9 Billion?

On June 2, 2021, Organon became a publicly traded company. It also became, on that same day, one of the most leveraged names in the pharmaceutical sector — carrying approximately $9.5 billion in debt before it had traded a single share. Of that total, $9.0 billion had already been wired to Merck as a special cash distribution. Organon entered the public markets not as a company that had borrowed to grow, but as one that had borrowed so its former parent could cash out.

This is the architecture of a leveraged spinoff: the subsidiary raises debt, pays the proceeds to the parent as a pre-separation dividend, and inherits the liability. It is entirely legal, tax-efficient for the parent under IRS Section 355, and — as Organon's trajectory would demonstrate — deeply consequential for everyone who stayed behind.

The $9 billion was not negotiated by Organon's management. It was the maximum amount Merck's bankers could place in the debt markets given Organon's projected cash flows — set before the new management team had any real authority to push back.

How the number was derived. The ceiling was determined by the intersection of three constraints: what the business could plausibly support in EBITDA, what leverage ratio the rating agencies would allow before forcing a below-investment-grade classification, and what interest rates made financially tolerable.

At 4× leverage on $2.2B of EBITDA, the arithmetic produced roughly $8.8–9.0B in serviceable debt. Any higher and the rating agencies would have required a high-yield designation, making the bonds harder and more expensive to sell. Any lower and Merck would have left value on the table. The bankers found the ceiling and recommended it.

What Merck did with the money. Three months after the spinoff closed, Merck announced its $11.5 billion acquisition of Acceleron Pharma. The Organon distribution was not earmarked for Acceleron in any public filing, but the timing is unambiguous: the leveraged spinoff was, functionally, a mechanism for Merck to monetize assets it no longer wanted in order to fund assets it did.

The three assumptions the model required. The $9 billion was defensible in June 2021 — but only under a specific set of conditions, all of which proved wrong, and none of which failed in isolation.

  • Broke
    Stable cash flows. Established Brands revenue was modeled as a gradual, predictable decline. Instead it was hit simultaneously by China VBP rounds, European loss-of-exclusivity on Atozet, and currency headwinds — compressing EBITDA margins from ~38% at spinoff to ~31% by 2025.
  • Broke
    Low interest rates. With the 10-year at 1.44% and all-in coupons at 4–5%, debt service was manageable. By late 2023 the Fed funds rate was at 5.25–5.50%. Organon's floating-rate term loans repriced upward, and the refinancing risk on its 2028 notes became a live question rather than a distant one.
  • Broke
    Growth paying down the debt. Biosimilars and women's health acquisitions were supposed to add cash flow that would de-lever the balance sheet over three to five years. Instead, each acquisition — Forendo ($75M upfront), Dermavant ($250M at close) — consumed the cash that should have been reducing the original $9B. Net leverage barely moved.
  • Broke
    Dividends as a secondary priority. At spinoff, Organon initiated a quarterly dividend of $0.28 per share — roughly $560M per year against ~502M diluted shares outstanding. The implicit assumption was that cash generation would be sufficient to service debt and reward shareholders. It was not. Over the 3.5 years from Q3 2021 to the 92% cut in May 2025, Organon paid out an estimated $1.8–2.0B in dividends while Net Debt / EBITDA barely declined from its opening 4× level. Every dollar paid to shareholders was a dollar not reducing the debt load that was slowly compressing the company's options.

None of these failures was unforeseeable in the abstract. What made the structure fragile was that it required all three to hold simultaneously. A company with 4× leverage, a declining revenue base, and no proprietary pipeline has no margin for error. Organon was given none.

The leveraged spinoff structure itself is not the problem — it has been used successfully many times. Two contemporaries make the contrast concrete. In July 2022, GSK demerged its Consumer Healthcare business as Haleon, loading it with £10.3B in net debt (~$13B) at ~3.6× opening leverage. In May 2023, Johnson & Johnson spun out its consumer division as Kenvue, raising $7.75B in bonds to pay J&J — but at only ~2.2× opening leverage against $3.7B of Adjusted EBITDA. All three spinoffs handed billions to their parents. Only one destroyed most of the equity value left behind.

The leverage rows tell the story on their own. Haleon entered at 3.6× and shed a full turn of Net Debt / Adj. EBITDA in three years. Kenvue entered at 2.2× and remained comfortably below 3×. Organon entered at 4.0×, barely moved for four years, and required a 92% dividend cut in 2025 just to stabilize.

The difference was not discipline — all three stated a commitment to deleveraging at separation. The difference was asset quality. Haleon's brands (Sensodyne, Panadol, Advil, Voltaren) carry no patent cliff, no biosimilar threat, no VBP exposure; organic revenue grew +11.6% in its first standalone half-year. Kenvue's brands (Tylenol, Neutrogena, Listerine) are similarly non-cyclical and defensible. Organon's largest segment — Established Brands, legacy Merck generics — faced simultaneous pressure from Chinese procurement reforms, European LOEs, and persistent FX headwinds from the moment it separated. The spinoff mechanism was the same across all three. The assets underneath were not.
